Today's headline

The format wars are over and HTML won. The debate isn't about markdown versus HTML anymore—it's about building outputs that AI agents can actually use. When you start thinking in handoffs instead of final deliverables, everything changes.

LINKEDIN DRAFTS

LI . NO. 01
Everyone's arguing markdown versus HTML.

That's not the debate.

The debate is: are you building outputs for people or handoffs for agents?

Because the second you start thinking in handoffs, everything changes.

Your output isn't the final thing anymore. It's the setup for the next thing. The scaffold. The structure that makes the next agent's job easier.

HTML wins that fight because it's richer. More interactive. More structured. Markdown is clean, but it's flat.

And flat doesn't work when the thing reading your output needs to make decisions.

So the real question isn't which format you like.

It's: what are you building for?
LI . NO. 02
Elastic just shipped something that matters.

Two new embedding models. Text, images, video, audio. Same vector space.

The part that matters: they share the exact same embedding space as their v5-text model.

Which means you don't rebuild your index. You swap the model. You start indexing images and video into the same vectors you're already using.

That's not a feature. That's a mental model shift.

Because multimodal search used to mean separate pipelines. Separate systems. Separate everything.

Now it's just search.

The pattern here is bigger than one tool. It's convergence. The stuff that used to be hard and separate is collapsing into one thing.

And when pipelines collapse, someone's business model breaks.

So the question is: are you building on the thing that's converging or the thing that's about to get absorbed?
LI . NO. 03
Anthropic's raising near a trillion.

Cerebras is upsizing their IPO.

TSMC is warning about capacity constraints.

Nobody's talking about what that actually means.

It means compute scarcity is coming. Not in five years. Soon.

The companies locking in fab capacity right now are making decade-defining bets. The ones waiting are going to be stuck fighting over scraps.

And this isn't just about chips. It's about who gets to build the next layer.

Because when compute is scarce, access becomes the moat. And the companies with access get to set the terms for everyone else.

So the move isn't just watching who raises money.

It's watching who's securing capacity while everyone else is still talking about models.
