LI . NO. 01
The speed wall for AI inference just moved.

Mercury 2 runs over 1,000 tokens per second on standard NVIDIA GPUs. The kind everyone already has. No custom chips. No proprietary hardware. No lock-in.

It runs on diffusion instead of the standard autoregressive approach, which means it generates tokens in parallel rather than one at a time. The result is 5-10x faster, quality comparable to Haiku or GPT-5 mini, at less than half the cost.

Augment Code ran it in production this week. 90% cost reduction. 82% latency improvement on real workloads.

Those aren't projected numbers. That's a team that had a problem and then solved it.

When inference gets that much cheaper at comparable quality, the features you said no to because the math didn't work start looking different. The calculus changes.

What did you shelve in the last year because running it at scale was too expensive?
LI . NO. 02
Your camera roll is a dataset you've been building for years and never read.

Every screenshot you saved meant something when you took it. A recipe. A product. A travel idea. A quote that hit.

Then it just sat there.

Pool launched this week. It takes your screenshots, runs AI across all of them, sorts them into categories, and makes them searchable. It can even trace a product screenshot back to the original retailer link.

What hits me about this isn't the app. It's the observation underneath it.

You've been passively curating a personal archive of your interests and intentions every time you grabbed a screenshot. You just never had a way to read it back.

That pattern applies way beyond screenshots. What are you already doing for yourself that someone else would find useful?

If it's repeatable, it's a skill. If it's searchable, it might just be a product.
LI . NO. 03
The quiet problem underneath all the agent hype is that nobody knows how to test them properly.

Julien Chaumond, CTO of Hugging Face, said this week that agentic AI evaluation is "massively under-resourced as a field."

Three words. 6.6K views. A full debate.

Here's what that actually means in practice.

When you ship a multi-step agent, you're asking it to take a sequence of actions over time. One wrong move cascades. Most teams don't have solid benchmarks for whether the agent is making the right call at each step.

So shipping reliable agents stays a game of trial and error.

That's not a research problem. That's a builder problem. Every team deploying agents right now is operating with less visibility into agent behavior than they should have.

The field is building fast. The infrastructure to verify it's working isn't there yet.

Worth factoring in before you go fully hands-off with agent workflows.
