LINKEDIN DRAFTS

LI . NO. 01
One builder racked up $5,000 in API costs in six weeks.

That's more than two years of Claude Max subscriptions. From one project.

The AI seat model is dying. Fast.

For the past couple years, flat-rate subscriptions covered what it actually cost to run these models. GitHub Copilot said it plainly last month: a quick chat and a multi-hour autonomous coding session cost the same. They've been absorbing the difference. That's over.

Anthropic went from $3B to $47B in annualized revenue in roughly a year. That math only works because people are burning tokens at a scale nobody planned for.

So here's the shift that matters: we're moving from "how much AI can I afford per month" to "how much does this specific workflow actually cost."

That's a different question. Most teams budgeted for AI like it was a software subscription. Agents changed the math.
LI . NO. 02
Anthropic published a 31.5% hijack rate for their browser agent.

Every other major lab gave us almost nothing comparable.

On the surface, 31.5% sounds bad. It isn't.

This spring, all four frontier labs put out prompt injection disclosures. Anthropic dropped 244 pages across four agentic surfaces. OpenAI covered one surface. Google moved the topic into a separate safety framework. Meta shipped nothing.

The 31.5% is the only real, comparable number anyone published.

And that changes what it means.

If I'm buying an enterprise AI tool and one vendor says "here's our actual red-team failure rate" and three others say "we take safety seriously," I know which one I trust with production data.

Showing your work in a space where everyone's racing to ship is genuinely differentiated. Not just honest. It gives buyers something real to evaluate.
LI . NO. 03
Robinhood now lets an AI agent buy stocks for you while your phone is off.

Google's Gemini Spark browses the web, checks your calendar, makes reservations. All in the background. Device off. No input needed.

Both shipped in the last week.

The question most people are asking is whether AI can do this. It clearly can.

The question that's actually interesting: do you trust it enough to hand it the keys?

Robinhood built in a kill switch and isolated accounts. Google requires approval before anything significant. Neither one is asking you to go all in.

They're offering a small, reversible experiment.

And that's how most agent adoption is going to happen. Not a big rollout. Not a policy decision. A kill switch and a spending cap and someone deciding "let's try it."

So: what's the smallest experiment you could run this week?
