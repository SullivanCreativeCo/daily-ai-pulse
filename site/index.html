OpenAI just shipped three voice models in one day.

GPT-Realtime-2. Real-time translation. Streaming transcription.

Not three separate products. One API release.

The thing nobody's talking about: these aren't call-and-response models. They're continuous dialogue models. You can describe a task, change your mind mid-sentence, and the agent keeps up.

That's not a feature. That's a different product category.

Because the old way was: you ask, it answers, you ask again. The new way is: you talk, it listens, it acts, the conversation never stops.

The moment this becomes real is when you stop thinking about "prompts" and start thinking about "conversations."

And the companies building for prompts are about to get left behind by the companies building for dialogue.

What's the first workflow you'd hand to a voice agent that never stops listening?
LI . NO. 02
Anthropic just taught Claude to dream.

Not in the poetic sense. In the "learn from your own mistakes while nobody's watching" sense.

Harvey saw task completion rates go up 6x after turning on dreaming. Wisedocs cut document review time in half. Netflix is running hundreds of builds in parallel.

The unlock isn't the model. The unlock is that the model can fix itself.

Because the problem with agents has always been trust. You can't put them in production if you have to check every output. And you can't scale if every mistake requires a human to step in and course-correct.

Dreaming solves that. The agent runs. It screws up. It notices. It adjusts. Next time, it doesn't screw up the same way.

That's not an agent. That's a system that compounds.

And the gap between "tool that needs supervision" and "system that improves on its own" is the gap between a nice demo and a real business.

What workflow are you not automating because you don't trust the agent to get it right every time?
LI . NO. 03
AWS just gave AI agents the ability to spend money.

Not hypothetically. Not in a demo. In production.

An agent can now connect to a paid service, see a 402 status code, read the payment instructions, complete the transaction, and get access. No human approval. No budget meeting.

The use cases are obvious. A financial analysis agent buys access to stock data. A coding agent subscribes to a dev tool. A research agent purchases a dataset.

But here's the thing nobody's saying out loud: if your service can't be purchased by an agent, you won't be in the consideration set by 2027.

Because the humans are already exhausted. They're not evaluating 47 SaaS tools to find the right one. They're telling an agent what they need and letting it figure out the rest.

And if your pricing page isn't agent-readable, if your signup flow requires a demo call, if your purchase process involves a human, you're out.

The question isn't "are agents going to buy from us?" The question is "are we set up for them to buy from us?"

Is your pricing page agent-readable?
