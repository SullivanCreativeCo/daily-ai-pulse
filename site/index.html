Today's headline

Fable 5 stopped being a tech story this week. It became a geopolitics problem.

Dario Amodei sat next to Emmanuel Macron at the G7 in France. Macron told reporters he made a "forceful plea" for the US not to keep frontier AI to themselves. Altman was there. Hassabis too. At one point Amodei was literally seated across the table from them.

The Fable shutdown taught every US ally the same lesson: the kill switch exists, the US holds it, and they actually used it.

That's a different conversation than it was two weeks ago.

LI . NO. 01
The most powerful AI model in the world went offline on a Tuesday afternoon.

Not crashed. Not rate-limited. Off. For everyone. Including paying enterprise customers who had built mission-critical workflows on it.

Here's what happened: the US Commerce Department sent Anthropic a letter. They couldn't verify passports at login. So they killed Fable 5 for everybody.

That was June 12th. It's still dark.

This was the first time in history that export controls were applied to an AI model. Not chips. Not hardware. The software itself.

The companies that got hit hardest are the ones with no fallback. No backup model. No parallel workflow.

So the question I keep coming back to: if your best AI tool went dark with four hours of notice, what breaks first?

Not trying to scare anyone. But the businesses figuring that out now are going to be in a very different position when the next one happens.

And there will be a next one.
LI . NO. 02
Three weeks ago, open-source models were the budget option.

This week they became the insurance policy.

When Fable 5 went offline, the companies with mission-critical workflows built on it had no fallback. The US government used export controls on an AI model. First time that's ever happened. Can't verify passports at login, so they killed it for everyone.

Bloomberg, Fortune, and CNBC all landed on the same conclusion: the biggest winner from the Anthropic situation is open source. The logic is simple. If you're running a model on hardware you control, nobody can send a letter and make it disappear.

The open models aren't a compromise anymore either. Z.AI just dropped GLM-5.2. Built entirely on Huawei Ascend chips. MIT license. No regional restrictions. Rivals Claude Opus on coding at about one-sixth the price.

The boring choice quietly became the smart one.

For anyone building AI workflows right now: what's running on hardware you control? Worth having an answer before you need one.

When the government bans something powerful, most people run.

Chris Koerner sees a buy signal.

Enterprise AI moves

  • Open Router's Fusion API is claiming Fable-level intelligence at half the cost - AI Daily Brief The setup: instead of one frontier model, Fusion fans out to multiple models in parallel, each with web search and tools. A judge model reads every response. A synthesizer writes the final answer. Open Router says their 100-task benchmark shows panels of models consistently outperform individual models. Whether it holds in practice is the real test, but companies that lost Fable access are very motivated to try it right now. https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4hvA6aCwf6E

  • Vercel shipped "eve" (open source agent framework) and "Passport" (shadow AI governance) - The Register Eve is TypeScript and Markdown, sandboxed VMs, fill-in-the-blanks agent building. Simple by design. Passport is the more interesting one for enterprises: it's a tool to bring rogue employee-built AI apps under company control. Gartner is reporting 59% of senior professionals have seen evidence of unsanctioned AI agents running inside their orgs. That problem needed a product. Now there's one. https://www.theregister.com/devops/2026/06/19/vercel-debuts-eve-open-source-agent-framework-tries-to-fix-shadow-ai-with-passport/5258726

  • Google, Microsoft, Cisco, Nvidia, and Salesforce proposed a new protocol for agents to find their own tools - InfoWorld It's called Agentic Resource Discovery (ARD). When an agent needs to pull from engineering docs, support tickets, deployment history, and observability systems, those all sit in different silos with no common layer. ARD is designed to be that layer. Spec is live now. If the big players actually adopt this, it changes how enterprise agent workflows get built at the foundation level. https://www.infoworld.com/article/4187305/solving-an-ard-problem-in-ai-agentic-resource-discovery.html
