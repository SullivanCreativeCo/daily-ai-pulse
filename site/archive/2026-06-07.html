Today's headline

Desktop AI agents crossed into the mainstream. Hermes, Tencent, and ManageEngine all shipped native UIs this week. The shift from "CLI only" to "click and drag" removes the infrastructure skill gate entirely. Non-technical builders can now deploy agents without touching code. That's the real story: not what agents can do, but who now gets to build them.

LINKEDIN DRAFTS

LI . NO. 01
The desktop agent boom happened this week.

Hermes dropped native apps. Tencent launched WorkBuddy for teams. ManageEngine rolled Zia Agents into their whole enterprise suite.

I'm watching something shift in real time.

Six months ago, agents lived in terminals and YAML files. You needed to know Docker, APIs, cloud infra. It was a skill gate.

This week, the skill gate disappeared.

Now you click. You drag files. You ask questions. The agent runs. You don't care where.

That sounds like a product feature. It's actually a story about who gets to build.

My take: non-technical people are about to ship agent-powered businesses. Your ex-boss who got laid off. The freelancer. The accountant. They're not learning APIs. They're solving problems their customers already have.

The constraint was never "what can agents do." It was always "who gets to build agents." That constraint just got removed.

So the real question isn't "are agents useful?" It's "what gets built when anyone can deploy one?"
LI . NO. 02
The move happened.

Boris Cherny, head of Claude Code at Anthropic, just said it straight: "I don't prompt Claude anymore. I have loops running that prompt Claude and figuring out what to do. My job is to write loops."

That's the inflection.

Six months ago, the skill was knowing how to prompt well. How to ask the right question. How to iterate.

That skill is being passed to the machine.

The new skill is writing the loop that writes the prompts that write the code.

You're not becoming a prompt engineer. You're becoming a systems designer.

Here's what that looks like in practice: you design feedback. The loop handles iteration. You step back and let it run until it hits a wall.

That's different from what most people are doing right now.

Most are still stuck in the "chat with Claude" phase. They prompt. They wait. They read output. They prompt again.

That works. But it's slow.

The move is to let the machine do the iteration.

Write the loop first. Then run it.
LI . NO. 03
Your frontier model isn't the win anymore.

Microsoft announced seven new MAI models. ManageEngine shipped Zia Agents for IT workflows. Alibaba pushed Qwen3.7-Plus for computer-use tasks.

None of them are using OpenAI.

They're not saying "we use GPT-5.5." They're saying "we built a model for this specific job."

That's the plot twist.

A 20B model trained on IT operations data beats GPT-5.5 at IT tasks. A 12B model trained on image understanding beats GPT-5.5 at image tasks.

It's not about size. It's about fit.

The frontier models are all-purpose. They're good at everything. They're optimized for nothing.

A domain-specific model is good at one thing. That one thing is what you actually need.

Here's the move: if you're building for an enterprise customer, you're not trying to fit them into OpenAI. You're building a model on their data.

Costs go down. Quality goes up. Your data stays yours.

Your ideas aren't bad ideas. They're just not finished ideas. The AI stack is finally giving you tools to finish them.

New harnesses & tools

Google Gemma 4 12B: Multimodal AI on 16GB Laptops — Explosion Google launched Gemma 4 12B with audio, video, image, and text inference on standard 16GB laptops. No cloud connection needed. Compression tech combines improved encoding and token prediction to run multimodal models on consumer hardware. Why this matters for thought leaders: if you're building solo or with a small team, you can now run multimodal inference locally for zero API cost. That changes the unit economics of any agent-heavy product. https://www.explosion.com/191228/google-gemma-4-12b-runs-locally-on-16gb-laptops-with-audio-and-video-ai/

Google Gemma 4 QAT: Frontier AI in Under 1GB — Awesome Agents Google DeepMind published quantization-aware training checkpoints for Gemma 4. The Gemma 4 E2B model now fits on smartphones and single-board computers in under 1GB. QAT isn't post-training compression. The quantization runs inside the training loop, so weights adjust to compensate for reduced precision. Result: higher quality than standard compression. Why this matters for thought leaders: capable AI inference just moved to devices with no internet connection. That's a new product category: offline-first agentic apps. https://awesomeagents.ai/news/gemma-4-qat-mobile-edge/

NVIDIA Nemotron 3 Ultra 550B: Hybrid Mamba-Transformer — Awesome Agents NVIDIA shipped Nemotron 3 Ultra (June 4, announced at Computex). 550B total parameters, ~55B active per token (MoE). 262K context in BF16, 1M in NVFP4. Architecture interleaves Mamba state-space layers with Transformer attention: Mamba handles long-context efficiency without quadratic cost, Transformer handles precise fact retrieval. Available on HuggingFace and OpenRouter at $0.50 per million input tokens. Intelligence Index: 48 (best US open model). Why this matters for thought leaders: long-context reasoning just got cheaper at scale. If you're building agents reasoning over 1M token contexts, this is your baseline. https://awesomeagents.ai/news/nvidia-nemotron-3-ultra-550b/

Alibaba Qwen3.7-Plus: Computer-Use Agent — WinBuzzer Alibaba launched Qwen3.7-Plus as a multimodal model for screen and coding automation. The model reads interfaces, chooses actions (click, type), writes code, and operates tools. Competes directly with Anthropic's computer-use agent. Why this matters for thought leaders: computer-use agents are now table stakes. Alibaba's entry signals the entire stack is commoditizing. The edge goes to teams that chain agents together, not teams that build one agent well. https://winbuzzer.com/2026/06/06/alibaba-pitches-qwen37-plus-as-a-computer-use-ai-agent-xcxwbn/

Harness-1: 20B Open-Source Search Agent Beats GPT-5.4 — Digg Patrick Jiang launched Harness-1, a 20B open-source search agent trained with state-externalizing harness.
