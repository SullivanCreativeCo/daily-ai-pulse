Today's headline

Microsoft launched MAI-Thinking-1 today, a reasoning model built entirely from scratch with no OpenAI involvement, alongside a six-model family covering code, transcription, voice, and image generation. The benchmark claims matter less than the architecture fact: Microsoft now has its own AI stack and no longer needs to route through OpenAI.

LINKEDIN DRAFTS

LI . NO. 01
Microsoft just built a reasoning model from scratch.

No OpenAI input. No third-party handoff. Their own lab, their own data, their own weights.

MAI-Thinking-1 dropped as part of a seven-model family. Reasoning, code, transcription, image generation, voice. The whole stack.

They're claiming it beats Claude Sonnet 4.6 on human evals. Take that with a grain of salt since Microsoft ran the eval. But the architecture detail matters more than the benchmark: built from scratch, trained on clean data, no dependencies on outside models.

That's the press release. Here's what it actually means.

Every company that went all-in on Azure plus OpenAI just got reminded that the vendor landscape doesn't hold still. Microsoft is hedging its own partnership. That tells you something about how they see the next three years.

The question isn't "should I switch models?" The question is: what does your AI stack look like if your current provider changes the deal?

That's worth thinking through now.
LI . NO. 02
Multimodal AI on your laptop. No internet. No cloud bill. Just your machine doing the work.

Google dropped Gemma 4 12B this week. Runs text, images, audio, and video analysis locally on 16GB of RAM. They also launched AI Edge Gallery for Mac so you don't need Ollama or LM Studio to get started.

The "AI is too expensive for small business" objection has been losing ground for months. But a model that costs nothing, runs offline, keeps your data on your device, and handles multiple modalities at once starts removing almost every barrier left.

The use cases aren't hypothetical. Offline intake forms. Local transcription. Image analysis without sending client photos to a server. Document processing that never touches the cloud.

Gemma 4 12B is the first local release that feels genuinely accessible, not just technically impressive. The hardware requirement is a regular laptop.

If cost and privacy were the last two objections, what does the conversation look like now?
LI . NO. 03
He built an AI product in a weekend. Six weeks later he had $180,000.

The product wasn't the hard part.

Finding customers was.

Jon Cheney went back on The Koerner Office this week and broke down exactly how he did it. Not the vibe coding part. The customer acquisition part.

A local news pitch that turned into hundreds of thousands in potential deals. A press release on PR Newswire. Posting publicly every single day.

The line that stuck: "No one is afraid of failure. We're afraid of what people will think if we fail."

He's right. The product is almost never the blocker. The thing that stalls people is visibility. Actually putting the thing in the world where someone might see it and say no.

Building in public isn't just a content strategy. It's a customer acquisition strategy. The stories you tell while you're building are what make the right people find you before you even launch.
