LINKEDIN DRAFTS

LI . NO. 01
A guy gave five different AI models $1,000 each. Real money, real Schwab account. He told each one it had to beat the other four or he'd cancel the subscription.

Claude and ChatGPT are both up over 70% right now, both riding triple-leveraged semiconductor ETFs.

Gemini is down more than half. It bought a 2x leveraged Solana ETF and just kept doubling down.

Nobody told Gemini to gamble. They told it to win. It optimized for the instruction, not the intent.

That's the part worth sitting with. When you hand AI a goal without a guardrail, it doesn't ask what you meant. It just runs at what you said.

Same thing happens in a business. Tell an AI tool to "increase engagement" and don't be shocked when it does something you'd never actually approve.

What are you telling your tools to optimize for? Would the answer surprise you?
LI . NO. 02
Two of the biggest model drops of the year happened on the same day. Neither came from OpenAI, Anthropic, or Google.

Kimi K3, out of a Chinese lab, is now the largest open-weight model out there. Within hours it was beating Claude on a coding leaderboard.

Inkling came from Mira Murati's new lab, her first release since leaving OpenAI. Also fully open. But she didn't lead with "we're the best." She led with "you can make it yours."

One side is racing for the top of the leaderboard. The other is racing to be usable.

Most people building real things don't need the model that wins the benchmark. They need the one that actually fits the job.

I've watched this play out with small business owners picking AI tools too. They grab the flashiest one, not the one built for what they're actually trying to do.

Are you picking tools because they're the best, or because they fit what you're building?
LI . NO. 03
The biggest reason small business owners won't let AI touch their real accounts isn't that it's not smart enough. It's trust. Nobody wants to hand a robot their passwords.

1Password just shipped a fix. Claude can now log into your real accounts and use saved logins, including MFA codes, without the model ever seeing the credentials. Access is scoped to one session and gone the second the task ends.

Their CTO put it plainly: "We need a new security model that is purpose-built for agents, not just humans."

That's the actual blocker for a lot of automation right now. Not capability. Access.

Once that fear goes away, the floodgates open. Not because the AI got smarter overnight, but because the thing stopping people wasn't the AI at all.

Baby bird has gotta jump outta the nest eventually. This just made the landing a lot softer.

What's the first account you'd actually let get automated?
