LI . NO. 01
The government just decided who gets to use the best AI models. Not based on capability or use case. Based on approval, one customer at a time.

Mythos approved for 100 institutions. GPT-5.6 in limited preview. Sam Altman published a memo saying this isn't their preferred approach.

This is the worst-case outcome. Labs still train at the same speed. It only slows public access. The gap between what's available publicly and what's locked internally widens from this day forward.

Everyone loses: safety advocates (this doesn't slow training), companies (artificial constraints), public (slower adoption), government (capricious control with no accountability).

The interesting move is what happens next. People are already talking about building around this—open models, routing layers, decentralized infrastructure. If you gate the best tools, people route around them.
LI . NO. 02
Stop building bigger models. Build smarter routers.

Sakana AI just shipped Fugu, an orchestrator that reads a query and routes it to GPT-5.5, Gemini, Claude, or composes them into a workflow. Not one model. One intelligent dispatcher.

Sail Research raised $80M to build infrastructure for long-horizon agents. Not for new models. For agents that run for hours or days, managing compute and cost across platforms that weren't designed for it.

The real power isn't in a model being 10% better. It's in dispatch, composition, and knowing which tool to use for which job. That's the move. One flagship model feels like yesterday's architecture.
LI . NO. 03
Your agent can't prove who authorized it, and that breaks everything.

Proof launched x401: an open protocol that lets services request and verify identity claims before permitting agent actions. Zero-knowledge proofs, so you verify without exposing full identity. Pairs with x402 for payments.

This is small and it's foundational. An agent that pays for something, publishes content, or makes a commitment needs proof-of-human-authority. We just tried to ship serious agent workflows without it.

Most infrastructure gaps are invisible until you hit them hard. This one's becoming visible fast. Whoever ships the trust layer for agents wins.

New harnesses & tools

  • Sakana AI launches Fugu (LLM orchestrator) - Sakana AI Stop thinking "one bigger model." Fugu reads each query and routes it to the right external model or combines them into a workflow. Not GPT-5.5. Not Gemini. Not Claude. The router picks the right one for the job. Trained with SFT, evolutionary strategies, and GRPO. This is the infrastructure layer that lets teams work around government gating.

  • Sail Research raises $80M for long-horizon agent infrastructure - Kleiner Perkins, Sequoia The thesis: agents need infrastructure built for hours or days of compute, not prompt-response rounds. This is the plumbing that'll support serious agentic workflows.

  • DeepReinforce ships Ornith-1.0 (coding model writes its own RL scaffolds) - DeepReinforce Open-weight family, 9B to 397B. The trick: model proposes its own scaffold for each task instead of using hand-written harnesses, then generates a solution against it. Reward flows back through both stages. Benchmark claims parity with Claude Opus 4.7. This matters if you're building with open models. https://aiinsiders.net/article/deepreinforce-ships-ornith-10-a-coding-model-that-writes

  • Proof launches x401 (identity protocol for agents) - Proof Agent that pays for something or publishes content needs to prove who authorized it. x401 is the open standard. Uses zero-knowledge proofs so you verify claims without exposing full identity. Pairs with x402 for payments. They're submitting to FIDO Alliance's agentic authentication standards group. This is the trust layer that's foundational but nobody noticed until agents started doing real things.
