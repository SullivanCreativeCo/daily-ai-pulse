LINKEDIN DRAFTS

LI . NO. 01
Three different companies shipped AI products today using almost the exact same line.

"From AI that advises to AI that executes."

Remote People's HR tool doesn't recommend salary changes. It makes them. Framer's agents don't mock up website edits. They publish them. Atlassian's Claude integration doesn't summarize a Jira ticket. It opens the pull request.

That's a real shift. And it's moving faster than most people realize.

The AI advisor era had a purpose. AI that answers questions, surfaces options, tells you what to do next. It's still useful. But the product market growing right now is AI that does the thing, shows you the result, and waits for your call.

If you're building something in this space, there's a real question worth asking: is your AI telling users what to do, or doing it for them?

One of those answers is getting harder to charge for.
LI . NO. 02
Vercel shared a number this week that I keep coming back to.

Six months ago, fewer than 3% of deployments on their platform were triggered by coding agents.

Now it's more than 50%.

That's not a prediction about where things are going. That already happened, quietly, while most of the discourse was still on whether AI-generated code was reliable enough to ship.

Vercel responded by shipping a full agent infrastructure suite. Agent Stack, Vercel Services, an open-source framework called "eve", enterprise agent tooling. Not because they saw it coming. Because the usage data was already there.

That's the tell. When a major platform provider builds infrastructure specifically for agent-triggered workflows, it means agents are already sending significant traffic.

For anyone building or deploying software right now, the question isn't whether agents will be part of the pipeline. They are. The question is whether your stack is built to handle that.

What does your deployment process look like today versus six months ago?
LI . NO. 03
There's a pairing showing up across completely different industries this week and I think it's worth naming.

AI does the analysis. A credentialed human does the judgment call.

ClearDiagnosis launched a medical second opinion service: AI reviews your records against national guidelines and research, a licensed physician reviews the findings and signs the report. $197. 24 hours. The normal path costs $2,000-$8,000 and takes weeks.

Coinbase structured their AI adviser inside an SEC-registered entity so it can give actual personalized investment recommendations, not just add a disclaimer to everything.

Atlassian's Claude agent does the implementation work. The developer reviews and merges.

Same shape. Different industries.

It makes sense when you sit with it. AI can process at a scale no human can match. The human brings accountability, credentials, judgment on edge cases.

If you're an expert in something, this is a frame worth sitting with: what changes if AI handles the volume and you handle the decisions?

That might not be a smaller job. It might be a better one.
