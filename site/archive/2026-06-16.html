LINKEDIN DRAFTS

LI . NO. 01
The gotcha feature is the whole product.

George Lampropoulos is 19 and just hit $200K in revenue building apps with AI. I watched his interview with Greg Isenberg and the insight stuck.

He spends 90% of his engineering time on one thing: the moment that explains the entire app in five seconds.

WrestleAI? Throw your wrestling match, get feedback. Done. Cal AI? Take a photo, get calories. That's it.

The reverse-engineer is the move here. Find the viral moment first. The one that makes someone stop scrolling. Then build the product around that moment.

Most builders do it backwards. Build the app, then figure out how to market it. George says: the gotcha moment is the market signal. If you can't explain the whole thing in five seconds, it's not a finished idea yet.

That's the product work. Everything else is distribution.
LI . NO. 02
Every business runs on the same five pillars.

I heard this from Corey Ganim today and it reframes how you sell AI to skeptics. The objection is always "my business is too specialized for AI." It's the objection that feels true.

It's not.

Corey breaks it down: sales, marketing, finance, operations, intelligence. Every business you'll ever touch runs those five systems.

The move is to map your workflows through those pillars. Not a generic template. But a first-principles framework that works for anyone.

That's how you kill the "too specialized" objection. Not by arguing. By showing.

The secondary move is positioning. You're not replacing people. You're automating tasks so people do the work they were hired for. That's the message that closes deals.

Find where you can automate a task in one of those five pillars. That's your entry point.
LI . NO. 03
Brad Liber has built 200+ custom homes and refused to build the same one twice.

People told him to scale. Replicate the best design. Stamp it across multiple lots. Make it predictable.

He turned that down every time.

His reasoning: a home that doesn't fit its land announces itself as a copy. That's not a Caliber home.

Twenty years later, people see a house in Denver and say "that's a Caliber home." They mean it as a standard.

That's what happens when you refuse to template.

The brand didn't come from marketing spend. It came from consistent refusal to compromise. From saying no to scale. From letting reputation compound slowly.

This applies to anything you're building. If you stop chasing everyone and keep the standard high, the right people find you.

That's harder than going viral. But it lasts longer.
