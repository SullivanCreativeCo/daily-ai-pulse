LI . NO. 02
Google just removed a wall.

Managed agents in the Gemini API. One API call. You write skills in markdown. The orchestration runs itself.

No framework debates. No debugging the handoff between agents. No infrastructure rabbit holes before you get to the actual thing you wanted to build.

Logan Kilpatrick demoed it at Google I/O by building an AI radio show that called seven different models. He didn't write a single line of orchestration code. Just markdown.

The barrier between "I want an agent that does X" and "here is an agent that reliably does X" just got a lot thinner.

Same harness that powers 900 million Gemini app users. Now accessible to any developer with an API key.

Your ideas aren't bad ideas. They're just not finished ideas. The tools to finish them keep getting cheaper and less technical.

What would you build if the hard part was already handled?
LI . NO. 03
Jeremy Howard posted something worth actually reading this week.

He called out Gemini Flash 3.5 for being fast and impressive but trained to chase benchmark scores instead of follow instructions. It goes off and does things "for" you that you didn't ask for.

His words: "The Claude models have consistently been better at this, and the market rewards that."

That's the model quality conversation most people skip over.

Benchmark numbers are easy to publish. Actual helpfulness in a real workflow is hard to measure. And right now, some of the highest-scoring models are also the most frustrating to actually work with.

If you're evaluating AI tools for your business, the score that matters isn't on any leaderboard. It's: does this model do what I asked, or does it do what it decided I meant?

Those are genuinely different things. One removes work. The other creates it.
