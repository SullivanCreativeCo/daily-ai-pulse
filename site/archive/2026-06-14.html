Here's the part that sits with me: everyone's been building on someone else's servers. Workflows, products, entire business logic running on cloud models. One letter and it evaporates.

Greg Isenberg had his whole weekend planned around building with Fable 5. Instead, he spent it learning why owning a layer of your stack isn't optional anymore.

Local models run on your machine. They're slower sometimes. They're not as smart sometimes. But they work when the cloud doesn't. They work when a government letter lands. They work offline, on planes, in bunkers.

The lesson isn't that cloud is bad. Cloud is still the smartest tool available.

The lesson is don't build your entire life on something you can't control.

So now the question is: what layer of your stack do you own?

Looks like token demand is collapsing.
Looks like the AI bubble is finally popping.

Except that's not what's happening.

What's actually happening is smarter.

Companies spent Q1 burning tokens like they were free. Walmart capped internal AI usage because demand exceeded budget. Uber blew through its quarterly token budget in four months.

Q2 they learned.

They learned to route queries to smaller models when they could. They learned to cache. They learned when you actually need the big brain versus when a smaller one works fine.

Demand didn't die.
Routing got intelligent.

This is the shift from "throw compute at it" to "be smart about where compute lives." And it changes how you build.

The era of "just make it run" is ending.
The era of "make it run efficient" is here.

That's not a bubble pop.
That's growth up.
LI . NO. 03
Databricks just released Omnigent.

It's a meta-harness. Sits one level above every harness (Claude Code, Codex, Pi, your custom agents). Treats each one as a swappable part.

Most engineers have four or five agents open right now. You're copy-pasting between them. Coding agent to search tool to Docs to Slack. Each harness only talks to itself.

Omnigent adds a layer where composition lives.

You build a team of agents. Mix harnesses. Control them with policies instead of prompts. Share live sessions instead of copy-pasting.

Here's what matters about this: the abstraction level jumped.

Last year the move was "which model should I use?"
This year the move is "which harness should I use?"
Next year the move is "which team of harnesses should I orchestrate?"

The people who get ahead aren't the ones picking the smartest single model.

They're the ones building the smartest composition.

That's a different skill.
