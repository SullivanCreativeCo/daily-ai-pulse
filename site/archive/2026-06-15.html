LINKEDIN DRAFTS

LI . NO. 01
AI made it trivially easy to ship products. The harder question now is knowing what to ship.

That's from Gokul Rajaram's launch post for Coach today. He built an AI reviewer for product docs because everyone in the org is building now. Engineers. Designers. Finance folks. Marketers. All moving at AI speed.

Ten people building in ten different directions at AI speed is a worse outcome than one person shipping the right thing slowly.

Coach is the tool he built to close that gap. You paste your PRD or strategy doc. It reads it the way a skeptical head of product would. Asks if the problem statement is actually a problem. Checks if your metrics measure outcomes or just activity. Flags the places where your roadmap is fiction.

The tool is useful. The idea underneath it is more useful.

We hit the part of the AI curve where taste and judgment are the moat. Speed was last year's answer.

What does that shift actually change for your team?
LI . NO. 02
Everyone's having the wrong conversation about AI models.

"Which model should I use?" is a 2024 question.

The 2026 question is: which model should handle which task?

Kimchi Coding became the first coding agent today to offer MiniMax M3, an open-weight model that performs at frontier level for a fraction of the cost. Interesting on its own. But the bigger story is what it signals.

The Wall Street Journal reported this month that enterprises are routing AI work across model mixes. Simple tasks go to cheaper open-weight models. The hard stuff gets the powerful commercial model. Cost drops. Performance holds.

That's not an enterprise-only play. It's available to anyone running AI in their business right now.

If you're treating AI like one tool for everything, you're overpaying for easy tasks and probably underpowered on the ones that actually matter.

The routing mindset fixes both.
LI . NO. 03
Carson and Jackson did $532,000 in one month flipping land they never owned.

Heard them on The Koerner Office today. The whole model: get the builder's buy box first. Know exactly what the buyer needs, what they'll pay, what geography, what lot size. Then go find landowners willing to sell for less and keep the spread.

No license. No degree. No capital to start.

They said if they lost everything tomorrow, they'd walk into a library, open Zillow, and be back at $20k a month within 30 days.

That level of confidence comes from one thing. A repeatable model that doesn't depend on any single tool or platform.

If it's repeatable, it's a skill. And if it's a skill, you can build on it.

Most people build the offer first and then try to find someone who wants it. Find the buyer's requirements first. The supply becomes obvious.

What are you already doing backwards?
