LINKEDIN DRAFTS

LI . NO. 01
Claude just executed a stock trade.

Not suggested one. Not analyzed one. Executed one.

Robinhood launched something called Agentic Accounts this week. You link an AI agent to a dedicated investment account. The agent builds your portfolio, rebalances it, places trades. While you're asleep or in a meeting or whatever.

Claude and Cursor are the first supported agents.

This is the part people keep glossing over. We spent two years debating whether AI could understand finance. Nobody stopped to ask when it would just start doing finance.

The line between AI as a tool and AI as an actor just moved. And it moved quietly.

Every product team building agents right now is going to face a version of this question. At what point does the agent get to act without a confirmation click?

What does your agent need access to before you'd trust it there?
LI . NO. 02
You can now tell Claude how hard to think.

That's not a metaphor. Anthropic dropped effort controls with Opus 4.8 this week. You pick low effort for quick tasks, high effort for the stuff that needs real reasoning. On higher settings the model thinks more frequently and more deeply. On lower settings it responds faster and burns through your rate limits more slowly.

That's a new variable most people haven't considered.

We've been obsessing over which model is smartest. The better question is: what is your task actually demanding? A quick email reply doesn't need the same compute as a codebase audit across 400,000 lines. Now you can match the effort to the job.

This also matters for cost. If you're running high-stakes API calls at full horsepower on tasks that didn't need it, you've been overspending.

Same model. Different settings. Very different outcomes.

What's the hardest task you're currently throwing at a model that could probably run on medium?
LI . NO. 03
This guy at ClickUp has 37 AI agents.

Scheduling agent. Analytics agent. Meeting transcript agent. One that talks to him like they're on the USS Enterprise. One with the personality of FBI negotiator Chris Voss for vendor calls.

I'm not saying you need 37. But the reason most people have zero isn't because agents are hard to build. It's because they're waiting to understand the whole system before they start.

The whole system is not the point.

Start with one task. The one you do every week that you could probably write down in two sentences. That's your job description. That's your first agent.

If it's repeatable, it's a skill. And if it's a skill, an agent can learn it.

The ClickUp guy didn't wake up one day with 37. He started with a scheduling agent because he kept planning his day the same way.

What do you keep doing the same way?
