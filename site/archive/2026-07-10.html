LI . NO. 02
Someone is selling a marketing operating system as a zip file.

That's the whole pitch.

Justin Brooke built CMO HQ - a full marketing and sales OS made entirely of folders and markdown files. Works with any AI. Customer buys from a sales page, gets a zip, hands the installer file to Claude or ChatGPT, and the AI builds the whole system for them.

No onboarding calls. No custom setup. No support tickets. Zero delivery work.

Here's what stuck with me: the product isn't the system. The product is the instructions. You build it once. The machine handles delivery forever.

'What are you already doing for yourself that someone else would find useful?'

Because if you've built a repeatable workflow for content, client onboarding, or lead follow-up, you've already built it. You're just not packaging it yet.

Is that a product? I think that's a product.
LI . NO. 03
Dan Shipper made a point this week that I haven't been able to shake.

Anyone can ship a first version of a software product now. One Codex session. Roughly 70% complete out of the gate. That's real, not hypothetical.

So if building the first version is easy, where does the value actually live?

Maintenance. The work of keeping it running, keeping it relevant, improving it week over week.

He built a live product called Turnaround to demonstrate this. A status badge that signals an app is actively maintained. The point isn't the badge. The point is that in a world where everyone can build fast, the moat is who shows up consistently.

Small businesses know this instinctively. A website that doesn't get touched is a dead website. A tool that doesn't improve gets replaced.

We moved from the 'build it' era into the 'keep it going' era. Are you building for that?
