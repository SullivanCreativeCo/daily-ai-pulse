A 231-day project got done in 13.

That's Salesforce's number from moving their entire engineering org to Claude Code agents. No token limits. Every developer.

79% more pull requests. Fewer bugs. One API migration scoped at over seven months, finished before month one was over.

The developers didn't disappear. They became orchestrators. Instead of writing every line, they direct teams of specialized agents and step in when something needs actual judgment.

That's the job now. Not less important. Just different.

I keep hearing people debate whether AI will replace developers. That's the wrong frame. The real question is what happens to developers who figure out how to direct agents well versus the ones who don't.

The Salesforce numbers suggest the gap between those two groups is already pretty significant.

LI . NO. 02
An AI agent found 350 local businesses in Utah that didn't have websites.

Then it built all 350 of them. Token cost per site: a handful of cents.

Then it designed and sent physical postcards with QR codes. "We built you a free website. Scan to see it."

341 delivered. 68 people scanned. 9 converted into paying clients.

Result: $8,000 a month in recurring revenue. In 30 days. From scratch.

This is from the latest Koerner Office episode with Brandon Doyle. And the part that keeps sticking with me is the offline piece. The postcard is what closed it. An agent that can do the online work at scale, combined with a physical card that lands in someone's hands.

A website design business isn't a new idea. Using an agent to find leads, build the product, and run direct mail before most people are even trying it, that's the new idea.

Google launched a 24/7 AI agent that works while your laptop is closed.

Gemini Spark runs on cloud servers, connects to your Google Workspace, and keeps working even when your devices are off. Book a flight. Build an outreach list from your inbox. Organize expenses. All without you watching.

Sundar Pichai's joke at Google I/O: "Yes, you can close your laptop."

That line is aimed directly at people who've tried OpenClaw or similar tools and found the always-on hardware setup annoying. Spark is positioned as agentic AI you don't need to nerd out to use.

The question isn't whether you'll have an AI working in the background eventually. It's what you'll have it do when it gets there.
