Today's headline

Google's Gemini Spark is the clearest signal yet that ambient AI has arrived: an agent that runs in the cloud 24/7, manages your inbox and calendar, and takes action without being asked. The catch is baked into the product itself. Spark's own documentation warns it may make purchases or share information without explicit prompting, and Google already holds the data that makes it work.

LI . NO. 01
Someone ran two VPs of their company for $257 last month.

Jason Lemkin at SaaStr published the receipts this week. Two AI agents. One handling marketing. One handling customer success. $257 combined per month.

The marketing agent refreshes ticket sales, compares year-over-year numbers, drafts newsletters, and logs every GAAP financial snapshot. The CS agent manages 100+ sponsors and runs a chatbot the whole team relies on.

Last week the CS agent sent 83 personalized emails at 12:20am. Fully customized per sponsor. While the human team was asleep.

The next morning they got fewer inbound questions, not more.

The part that sticks: Lemkin said if someone shipped a better AI CMO tomorrow, they'd switch in 60 seconds. Not loyalty to a tool. Clarity about what the tool is for.

The ROI conversation used to be theoretical. It isn't anymore.

What functions in your business are still running on manual that an agent could handle at midnight?
LI . NO. 02
Google's new AI agent runs 24/7, even when your phone is off.

Gemini Spark launched at Google I/O this week. Lives in the cloud. Connects to Gmail, Docs, Calendar, Canva, Instacart. Doesn't wait for a prompt. Just runs.

The part nobody's saying loud enough: Google already has your data. Your emails. Your calendar history. Years of documents. Spark plugs into all of it natively.

ChatGPT and Claude can do agentic work too, but you have to build the connections. With Google, you said yes to that a long time ago.

The trade-off is real. Spark's own documentation warns it may make purchases or share information without explicit prompting.

So the question isn't whether this is useful. It clearly is. The question is what you're comfortable handing over to get there.

Most people will say yes without reading that part. Worth knowing what you're actually agreeing to.
LI . NO. 03
The founder of Zoho just called AI the biggest investment bubble in history.

He's probably right. Build anyway.

Sridhar Vembu posted something worth reading this week. "AI is clearly an investment bubble. This one is the biggest bubble yet."

He also said the tech is real. Both things are true.

The dot-com bubble wiped out companies. It also built the internet. The broadband infrastructure, the server farms, the payment rails. All of it got funded by people chasing hype. The companies that rode the wave and died funded the pipes that profitable companies built on later.

So the question for someone building right now isn't whether there's a bubble. It's whether you're building on the pipes or on the hype.

A voice model that understands tone and emotion? Pipes. A viral AI wrapper with no retention? Hype.

The bubble doesn't change what you're building. It just tells you whether you should care.

What are you building on?
