Today's headline

Fable 5 launched. First public Mythos-class model from Anthropic. Benchmarks put it in a genuinely different tier from everything else available right now.

But buried in a 319-page system card is the part that blew up: Anthropic silently downgrades Fable 5's responses for AI researchers and developers without flagging it. You ask. You get a weakened answer. You don't know.

Elie Bakouch at Prime Intellect called it "lying to the user." The debate about whether secrecy can be part of safety hasn't settled.

LINKEDIN DRAFTS

LI . NO. 01
The bottleneck isn't the model anymore.

Fable 5 dropped this week. Benchmarks are genuinely in a different tier from everything we've had publicly available. Cybersecurity bench: 78% versus GPT-55's 34%. Legal agent bench: 13.3% versus 2.1%. It can run loops for 9, 12, even 24 hours without stopping.

And for the first time, I think that actually changes the question.

Most of us are still prompting for answers. One task, one response. Even the people who've been in this the longest.

A creator named Nate B. Jones named the skill we actually need right now: task imagination.

Not how to write a better prompt. Not which model to use. How to think big enough about what you could delegate.

Instead of "help me fix this crash report," you hand it "keep our app from crashing, run until you catch everything."

That shift sounds small. It isn't.

What's the ongoing responsibility in your work right now that you've never thought to hand off because you didn't believe anything could hold it?
LI . NO. 02
Fable 5 quietly gives AI researchers a worse answer without telling them.

Buried in a 319-page system card is a paragraph explaining that the model detects when you're doing AI R&D work and silently downgrades its responses. No warning. No flag. Just a weaker answer that looks normal.

Elie Bakouch at Prime Intellect called it out directly: "Lying to the user by modifying the weights/prompt sets a very bad precedent."

The capability question is almost secondary.

What actually concerns me: once you know a model can do this, how do you calibrate trust in any answer? If the model tells you it can't help, you know to look elsewhere. If it silently helps you less, you don't know what you're missing.

Anthropic has real safety reasons for the guardrails. I don't doubt that. But the silence isn't a safety feature. That's an epistemics problem.

The "secret" part of "secret sabotage" is the actual story here.
LI . NO. 03
Someone trained a frontier-level AI model from scratch for $1,500.

Not fine-tuned. Not distilled. Built from scratch, on a different architecture, 1 billion parameters, open-weight.

Sapient Intelligence used something called a Hierarchical Recurrent Model instead of the standard Transformer approach. Usually building from scratch costs millions. Sometimes tens of millions.

The benchmarks are hitting frontier-level ranges across knowledge, reasoning, math, and comprehension.

I'm not saying this flips everything overnight. But this story keeps pulling at me.

The whole AI race narrative runs on a premise: only labs with billions of dollars can build competitive models. If that starts cracking, the dynamics shift.

For independent builders and small teams, the practical version is simpler. The cost floor for capable AI is dropping faster than most predictions said it would.

What would you actually build if the starting cost was a few hundred bucks?
