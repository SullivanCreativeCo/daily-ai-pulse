OpenAI just made GPT-5.5 Instant the default model for every ChatGPT user.

Not a beta.
Not a preview.
The default.

That means tighter responses. Stronger factual accuracy. Better personalization through memory sources that show you exactly what context shaped the answer.

Plus and Pro users get the full upgrade now—past chats, files, connected Gmail all feeding in. Free and Enterprise users get it in weeks.

The thing is, this isn't about a model upgrade.

LI . NO. 02
Google killed Project Mariner this week.

You remember Mariner. The autonomous browsing agent that could navigate Chrome, fill out forms, book travel—all by processing screenshots in real time.

They announced it at I/O last year. Shut it down May 4.

WIRED reported two months ago that Google moved staff off the Mariner team to respond to OpenClaw-style agents. Now we know why.

The shutdown isn't about agents failing. It's about Google pivoting to a different architecture.

Screenshot-based browsing worked. But it was slow. Prone to errors. And it required massive compute for visual processing at scale.

Meanwhile, OpenClaw-style agents are shipping and iterating fast.

The real story here isn't what Google killed. It's what they're building instead.

When a company with Google's resources backs away from a high-profile project that fast, it means they saw something better coming.
LI . NO. 03
Anthropic just taught Claude to dream.

Not a metaphor. A literal feature called Dreaming.

Here's how it works: between your active sessions, Claude reviews everything it did. Past conversations. Logged memory. Recurring mistakes. Workflow patterns.

Then it updates its own memory.

This is the first major agent memory upgrade that happens while the agent is offline.

Think about that.

Most agents forget everything between sessions. You start fresh every time. You repeat yourself. The agent repeats its mistakes.

Dreaming changes that.

Now the agent learns from what it got wrong yesterday. It refines its approach while you sleep.

This is what separates a tool from a partner.

A tool needs instructions every time. A partner remembers what worked and what didn't.

If you're building agents for long-running work, this is the unlock.
