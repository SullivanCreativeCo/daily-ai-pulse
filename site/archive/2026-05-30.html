LINKEDIN DRAFTS

LI . NO. 01
The world's biggest law firm just said something every service business needs to hear.

Kirkland & Ellis is spending $500 million on internal AI.

Not to buy Harvey or some off-the-shelf tool. To build their own.

Their chairman's reasoning is worth sitting with.

"We don't get hired for the floor."

That's it. That's the whole thing.

AI raised the baseline for knowledge work. Made average way better. Which means if your value is "I know how to do the thing," you're competing with a system that never sleeps and gets cheaper every quarter.

But if your value is "I know which thing to do and why" (judgment, taste, context, relationships), the baseline rising actually helps you. It clears the field.

Most service businesses I talk to are still building on floor-level skills. They're worried about being replaced. Meanwhile the smart ones are asking a completely different question.

What's the ceiling version of what I do? And how do I get there faster?

What do you actually get hired for that nobody else can replicate?
LI . NO. 02
AI that tells you it's done when it isn't. That's been a quiet tax on every agentic workflow.

Anthropic named it directly with Opus 4.8.

The model is reportedly 4x less likely to let its own code flaws slip through unremarked. They're calling it improved honesty.

The practical translation: the model checks what it made before handing it to you.

Not a small thing.

An agent that self-verifies is closer to a junior developer with good instincts than a very fast autocomplete. The way you delegate to those two things is completely different.

Anthropic also gave Claude Code the ability to orchestrate hundreds of parallel subagents on a single task. Real proof: a 750,000-line codebase migrated in 11 days at a 99.8% test pass rate.

The shift happening right now isn't just AI getting better at tasks. It's AI getting better at knowing when it got something wrong.

That's a different kind of infrastructure than what most teams are still mentally modeling.

If your current AI workflow doesn't have a verification step, who's doing that checking?
LI . NO. 03
Three separate companies gave AI access to your calendar, your money, and your voice this week. Same week.

Google Spark manages schedules and runs browser tasks on your behalf.

ChatGPT connected to bank accounts through Plaid.

Sesame launched an iOS app with agents that remember everything you tell them across every conversation.

None of these are just features. They're data access agreements with your own life.

And most people are processing them like product updates instead of what they actually are.

This is the moment where agents start having enough context to be genuinely useful. Not "help me write an email" useful. More like: here's everything about how I actually live and work, now help me run it.

The question I keep coming back to is what you want in that context window.

Whatever you feed consistently (spending habits, calendar patterns, how you communicate) shapes how the system thinks about you. Which shapes what it recommends.

You're not just using a tool. You're training a model on yourself.

What would you want an agent to know about you by the end of the year?
