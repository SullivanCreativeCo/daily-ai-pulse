LINKEDIN DRAFTS

LI . NO. 01
The most interesting AI conversation right now isn't about what AI can do.

It's about what it can't.

Chris Koerner dropped an episode about a $60K/month side hustle that AI structurally won't replace. The title alone stopped me mid-scroll.

Because here's what I keep seeing. Everyone's racing to build AI-adjacent things. Automations, agents, wrappers. Which, fine. I do that too.

But there's a whole category of work that gets more valuable as AI gets better. Not because it's complicated. Because it's human.

Physical. Relational. The kind of thing where showing up matters more than being smart.

The people I've seen win consistently aren't the ones with the best tech stack. They're the ones who picked a lane where being a real person in the room is the whole product.

That's not going away. If anything, it's getting rarer.

Worth asking: what are you already good at that a model genuinely can't do?
LI . NO. 02
AI tools will not save a business that has no real value underneath.

I see this constantly. Someone builds a slick automation. Cleans up their workflow. Gets really good at prompting.

And they're still not making money.

Because the AI was layered on top of nothing. A vague service. A product nobody asked for. An idea that felt good but hadn't been tested.

The tool isn't the business. The tool is what you use once the business is real.

Chris Koerner has been saying this in different ways for years. Business model first. Offer has to work. Then you bring in the tools.

Most people skip that part because AI makes everything feel possible. And it is possible. But possible and profitable aren't the same sentence.

What's the actual thing you're selling?
LI . NO. 03
$60K a month.

That's the number Koerner used in a recent episode title. Not "six figures." Not "scale your income." Sixty thousand dollars per month.

That's a choice. The specificity is intentional.

Because vague aspiration is everywhere. "Build a business." "Create passive income." "Grow your audience." Every other post is doing that.

The people who actually help you do something are the ones who get granular. They name the thing. They give you the number. They explain why it works.

AI has made it easier than ever to produce content that sounds valuable but says nothing. Generic advice. Repackaged frameworks. Beautifully formatted emptiness.

The counter-move is specificity. Real numbers. Real stories. Real failures.

That's what cuts through.

If you're creating content right now, or building a service, or trying to explain what you do, the question is: how specific can you actually get?

Not "I help businesses grow." What does that mean? What did you actually do?
