LI . NO. 02
Capital One just gave away the security tool they built to protect Capital One.

They built VulnHunter on Claude Opus and Claude Code. It maps how an attacker would actually reach exploitable code, then runs a second pass that kills half the false alarms before a human ever sees them.

Then they put it on GitHub. Apache 2.0. Free.

A bank doesn't give away its crown jewels by accident. They gave it away because the tool was never the moat. The moat was everything they learned running it across thousands of repos first.

This is the same move I keep telling small business owners to make. Stop guarding the how. Nobody's paying for your process anyway. They're paying for the judgment behind it, the years of "we already tried that and here's what actually broke."

If you open sourced your process tomorrow, would you still have a business? If yes, you've got something real. If no, you've got a secret, and secrets don't scale.
LI . NO. 03
OpenAI just released a $230 keyboard. That's not the story.

Codex Micro. 13 mechanical switches, a joystick, RGB lights that change color depending on what your AI agent needs from you. Built with a luxury keyboard company.

Feels like a gag gift. It's not.

A year ago most people ran one coding agent and watched it like a hawk. Now builders are running four, five, six agents at once, each chewing through a different part of the codebase, and the bottleneck isn't the AI anymore. It's you, tabbing between windows trying to remember which agent needs an answer.

So somebody built a physical dashboard for babysitting AI. That's the actual signal. The work moved from "can the model do this" to "how many of these can one person manage at once."

Nobody was managing a fleet of agents in 2025. Now there's hardware for it.

What does your job look like when you're managing five of you instead of doing the work of one?
