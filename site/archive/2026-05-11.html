LINKEDIN DRAFTS

LI . NO. 01
OpenAI just unbundled real-time voice into three separate models.

GPT-Realtime-2 for reasoning. GPT-Realtime-Translate for live multilingual calls. GPT-Realtime-Whisper for transcription only.

The move isn't about features. It's about pricing granularity.

Most live voice workflows don't need GPT-5-class reasoning on every utterance. They need fast transcription with occasional escalation to the expensive model when stakes justify it.

Unbundling lets you run cheaper inference where it's enough and route complex questions to heavy reasoning only when the math works.

Microsoft and xAI already have real-time voice agents in market. So the question isn't "can OpenAI do live voice?"

It's "does splitting three products reduce orchestration tax enough to beat a competitor's single API?"

The answer probably depends on how messy your workflow already is.
LI . NO. 02
OpenAI's new Codex Chrome extension lets the agent operate inside your signed-in browser session.

Gmail. Salesforce. LinkedIn. Internal dashboards. The stuff APIs don't cover.

The reason this matters: most enterprise workflow lives in authenticated web apps. Structured integrations miss the live account state. The forms. The admin panels. The internal tools that never got an API in the first place.

Codex uses tab groups, host prompts, and approval gates instead of unrestricted browser control. So you're not just handing it the keys. You're handing it supervised access with a checklist.

The risk surface is obvious. You're giving an agent your logged-in session. Regional rollout limits and admin policy will decide adoption speed faster than the feature itself.

But the automation prize is bigger than most integration catalogs can reach.

So the question isn't "is this safe?" The question is "what are you already doing manually in a browser that you'd pay to automate if someone built the guardrails right?"
LI . NO. 03
Alation just launched AI Governance and the timing is sharp.

The product creates a single system of record for every AI model, agent, and tool in the enterprise. Live compliance posture for boards and regulators on demand.

The problem it solves: most CDOs spend weeks manually assembling evidence when asked about AI compliance. Approval workflows live in email threads and SharePoint pages. Model documentation goes stale the day it's filed.

There is no system of record for AI approvals. And regulators are no longer willing to wait for one.

Alation registers every AI asset into one inventory. Maps approvals, lineage, and policy adherence. Then generates board-ready reports without the scramble.

The strategic bet: enterprises are deploying AI faster than they can govern it. The ones who solve governance infrastructure first get to move faster while everyone else is stuck in manual audit hell.

So here's the question: if a regulator asked you today to show your AI approvals, how long would it take you to produce the answer?
