LI . NO. 01
The frontier labs went quiet in May.

No new GPT drop. No Opus update. No Chinese lab trying to one-up the leaderboard.

But the architecture conversation got loud.

MiniMax open-sourced a 456B-parameter model that doesn't use Transformers. They call it Lightning Attention. 4 million tokens of context. 20x longer than the leading models.

Thinking Machines—Mira Murati's post-OpenAI lab—shipped interaction models that don't do turn-based chat. They process continuous audio-visual streams in 200ms micro-turns. You talk, the model listens and responds at the same time. It translates while you're still speaking. It notices when someone new walks into frame.

This isn't a feature bolted onto GPT. It's a different training paradigm.

When the capability race pauses, the infrastructure conversation fills the void.

For two years, the playbook was "just scale the Transformer." More parameters, more data, more compute. Now we're seeing the first credible alternatives at frontier scale.

The question isn't "who has the biggest model" anymore.

It's "what can you do with a model that processes the world differently?"

Anthropic launched a consulting joint venture with Blackstone, H&F, and Goldman Sachs last week.

Anthropic also shipped Claude for Small Business—a packaged version with 15 prebuilt workflows for QuickBooks, PayPal, HubSpot, and other tools small businesses actually use.

The frontier labs are done pretending the model sells itself.

Enterprise adoption is a services business now. The model is table stakes. The deployment layer is the moat.

OpenAI's DeployCo raised $4B at a $10B pre-money valuation. Word is the investing partners bought in to skip the line—get first access to forward-deployed engineers for their portfolio companies.

Anthropic's small business package is explicit about it: "We're targeting firms that use QuickBooks and Canva because they're lagging on AI adoption and they need workflows, not benchmarks."

This is the Palantir view taking over.

The money isn't in shipping the smartest model. It's in being unavoidable inside institutions.

If you're running an AI agency or consulting practice, the question isn't "am I competing with OpenAI now?"

It's "how do I serve the longtail they'll never reach?"

Freshworks just released a no-code builder for AI agents.

Anthropic shipped 15 prebuilt workflows for small businesses.

Google is reframing Android as an "intelligence system" instead of an operating system.

The bottleneck isn't coding anymore.

It's describing the workflow clearly enough for the system to understand it.

Every week, someone ships a new no-code agent builder. Freshworks calls theirs Freddy AI Agent Studio. You can spin up custom agents, deploy them into Slack or Teams, connect them to your HRIS, and run workflows like onboarding or asset requests without writing a line of code.

The new Zapier is "I can describe what needs to happen, and the builder figures out how to make it happen."

This changes who can build AI solutions.

You don't need a developer anymore. You need someone who understands the workflow well enough to explain it.

If you're advising teams on AI adoption, the question isn't "do we have engineers who can code this?"

It's "do we have someone who can map the process clearly enough for the no-code builder to execute it?"

That's a different skill. And it's suddenly the most valuable one in the room.

AI launches & news

  • MiniMax-01 series goes open-source with Lightning Attention — MiniMax Chinese lab MiniMax released MiniMax-01, a 456B-parameter foundation model (45.9B active per inference) trained on a novel Lightning Attention mechanism instead of traditional Transformers. The model handles 4 million tokens of context—20 to 32 times longer than leading models—and matches frontier performance while running on a fundamentally different architecture. This is the first large-scale implementation of Lightning Attention in production. https://www.minimax.io/news/minimax-01-series-2

"For the first time on a large scale, we have implemented a novel Lightning Attention mechanism, offering a new alternative to the traditional Transformer architecture."

  • Thinking Machines ships interaction models for real-time human-AI collaboration — AI Daily Brief Mira Murati's post-OpenAI lab released interaction models trained from scratch to handle continuous audio-visual exchange instead of turn-based chat. The architecture splits into two parts: a real-time interaction model processing 200ms micro-turns and a background reasoning model handling long tasks. Demos show simultaneous translation while someone is still speaking, dialogue management that tracks when a speaker is yielding or thinking, and visual cue recognition when someone new enters frame. This is not a feature bolted onto GPT—it's a different training paradigm. https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-UTIXsziBJI

  • OpenAI launches GPT-5.5-Cyber under restricted access program — OpenAI OpenAI released GPT-5.5-Cyber in limited preview for verified defenders working on critical infrastructure. The model is trained to be more permissive on security-related tasks—exploit validation, red-teaming, proof-of-concept work—while restricting requests that could enable real-world harm. Access runs through the new Trusted Access for Cyber framework, which gates capability behind identity verification, organizational approval, and authorized-use scoping. https://openai.com/sl-SI/index/gpt-5-5-with-trusted-access-for-cyber/

Enterprise AI moves

  • Anthropic launches Claude for Small Business with 15 ready-to-run workflows — Anthropic Anthropic shipped Claude for Small Business, a packaged version of Claude with connectors to QuickBooks, PayPal, HubSpot, Canva, Docusign, Google Workspace, and Microsoft 365. It includes 15 prebuilt agentic workflows for tasks like payroll planning, invoice chasing, campaign creation, and month-end close. This is Anthropic moving down-market with workflow packaging instead of frontier-model evangelism. The company paired the launch with training, nonprofit partnerships, and a roadmap explicitly framing small businesses as a lagging adoption segment. https://www.anthropic.com/news/claude-for-small-business

    Why this matters for thought leaders: The shift from "buy our model" to "buy our workflows" is the acknowledgment that adoption is harder than capability. When frontier labs start looking like consultancies, that's the signal that enterprise inertia matters more than benchmark scores.

  • OpenAI formalizes DeployCo with $4B investment and Tomoro acquisition — OpenAI OpenAI launched the OpenAI Deployment Company (DeployCo), a separate joint venture with 19 partners including TPG, Advent International, Bain Capital, and Brookfield. The company raised $4B at a $10B pre-money valuation and is acquiring AI consulting firm Tomoro to bring 150 forward-deployed engineers on day one. Word on the street: investing partners bought in to skip the line for their portfolio companies. Goldman Sachs is the only firm backing both DeployCo and Anthropic's unnamed consulting venture. https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-UTIXsziBJI

New harnesses & tools

  • Freshworks unveils Freddy AI Agent Studio with no-code builder — SiliconANGLE Freshworks released Freddy AI Agent Studio, a no-code environment for building, customizing, or deploying prebuilt AI agents across IT service workflows. The platform includes a Model Context Protocol Gateway for connecting Freddy AI to third-party tools and a new analytics layer called AI Insights with Experience Level Agreements. Freshworks is pitching this as a unified foundation that lets customers move agents from pilots to production in weeks instead of quarters by tying service, asset, and incident data into a single layer. https://siliconangle.com/2026/05/14/freshworks-unveils-freddy-ai-agent-studio-mcp-gateway-freshservice/

    Why this matters for thought leaders: No-code agent builders are becoming the new Zapier. If you're advising small teams on AI adoption, the question isn't "can you code" anymore—it's "can you describe the workflow clearly enough for the builder to understand it."

  • Google teases Googlebook range to replace Chromebooks — Techmeme Google is killing the Chromebook and replacing it with Googlebook, a new laptop lineup powered by Android and Gemini Intelligence. The company is reframing Android from an operating system into an "intelligence system" where AI is the core interaction model, not a feature. Rollout starts on select Samsung Galaxy and Google Pixel devices this year, with broader device availability coming later. Acer, ASUS, Dell, HP, and Lenovo are all shipping Googlebooks this fall. https://www.techmeme.com/260513/p39

    Why this matters for thought leaders: When the OS becomes the intelligence layer, the computer stops being a tool and starts being a context-aware agent. If you're building thought leadership around AI workflows, you're about to have a much larger addressable audience who didn't choose AI—it just came with their laptop.

  • Poppy launches proactive AI assistant to organize digital life — TechCrunch New app Poppy combines calendar, email, messages, and other sources into a single dashboard, then uses AI to guess what's important based on what's going on in your life. Users connect services like email, calendar, and location, and Poppy surfaces relevant meetings, tasks, and context in widgets or its app. This is the "AI personal assistant" category maturing past chatbots into proactive context engines. https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/13/poppy-debuts-a-proactive-ai-assistant-to-help-organize-your-digital-life/

    Why this matters for thought leaders: The shift from "ask the AI a question" to "the AI knows what you need before you ask" is the beginning of ambient intelligence. If you're writing about productivity workflows, the new baseline is tools that anticipate instead of react.
