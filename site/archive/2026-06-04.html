LI . NO. 02
Anthropic filed IPO paperwork this week at a $965 billion valuation.

Same week Bernie Sanders introduced the AI Sovereign Wealth Fund Act.

He wants the federal government to take a 50% stake in the foundation labs, pay dividends to US citizens, and eventually fund healthcare, education, and housing from AI profits.

These two things happening in the same news cycle isn't an accident. They're the same conversation from opposite ends.

Here's the part most people are sleeping on: OpenAI's own April white paper called for a public wealth fund that gives every citizen a stake in AI-driven economic growth.

The labs know this debate is coming. They just want to be the ones writing the frame before Congress does it for them.

The question of who owns the upside from AI isn't going to stay in think-pieces and Senate proposals. It's going to end up on a ballot, in a courtroom, or in a merger agreement.

If you're building in AI right now, this is the policy environment you're going to be building inside.

Worth understanding what each side actually wants.
LI . NO. 03
The AI that used to require a data center now fits on a laptop.

Google dropped Gemma 4 12B today. Runs on 16GB of VRAM. Handles text, vision, and audio natively in one model. No separate encoders stitched together.

That's not just a size win. It's an architecture win.

For two years the local AI story was mostly for enthusiasts. The useful stuff lived in the cloud because the hardware wasn't there.

Now Nvidia is shipping a chip this fall called RTX Spark positioned as direct competition to Apple's M-series. One petaflop of AI compute in a Windows laptop. Apple has been the only serious choice for local inference. That's about to be contested.

What this opens up for builders: privacy-first products, offline-first products, on-device products where latency is zero and data never leaves the device.

A lot of use cases that weren't viable 18 months ago are about to make obvious sense.

What would you build if the AI never had to phone home?
