LINKEDIN DRAFTS

LI . NO. 01
The SEO playbook you've spent years on is about to need a partner.

Not a replacement. A partner.

Greg Isenberg put out a tight 14-minute episode this week that reframed how I think about the next decade online. The premise: AI agents are becoming the customer. They find products, evaluate options, transact, and recommend to other agents.

They don't read your hero copy. They don't click your CTA. They call your API, check your docs, and look for an OAuth endpoint.

If an agent can't figure out what you do, you're invisible to it.

That's not a future problem. AgentMail is already building inboxes for AI agents. Stripe has an agent wallet. Codex has 5 million weekly users building and deploying tools every week.

The question worth asking right now: is my website agent-readable?

Structured docs. A /agents entry point. MCP tools. Not complicated. But it requires knowing the question exists.

Most people don't know the question exists yet. That's the window.
LI . NO. 02
Microsoft named a new product category at Build 2026 yesterday and I think that matters more than the product itself.

They're calling it "Autopilots."

Not agents you prompt. Not chatbots you supervise. Things that just run.

Microsoft Scout connects to your Teams messages, Outlook, calendar, OneDrive, and does the coordination work you keep putting off. Scheduling across time zones. Flagging stalled decisions. Blocking calendar time before a deadline sneaks up on a Tuesday. Without being asked.

It's built on OpenClaw, the open-source agent framework that hit 180,000 GitHub stars in three months after launching in January. Microsoft saw what the community proved and wrapped it in enterprise security. Open-source proves the concept, platform adds compliance and distribution. That's the playbook now.

Here's what's interesting for anyone running a team or a business: an Autopilot isn't a feature you learn to use. It's a system you give permission to.

That's a different kind of decision. What does your organization need to think through before something acts on your behalf without being asked?
LI . NO. 03
The math on Corey Ganim's AI consulting offer is embarrassingly simple.

Two 45-minute calls a month. A walkie-talkie app for async questions. A shared Notion doc where everything lives.

That's the whole product. $1,500 a month. $1,000 effective hourly rate.

He doesn't do the work for clients. He does it with them. They share the screen. They build Claude skills together. They fix the process before they automate it.

The infrastructure took two hours to set up once. Two Claude skills auto-fill the call notes and draft the follow-up email after every session. Post-call admin: 30 seconds.

Started at $1,200/month. Raised to $1,500. Now $2,000. His logic for raising every time: when you're closing 100% of your sales calls, you're priced too low.

Cap: six clients.

"What are you already doing for yourself that someone else would find useful?" That question has a dollar sign on it. Corey found his answer. The model's there if you want to run it.
