LINKEDIN DRAFTS

LI . NO. 01
One guy at Commerce just decided who gets to use the best AI.

No vote. No executive order. Howard Lutnick sent a letter giving about 100 trusted organizations access to Anthropic's Mythos again.

Then GPT-5.6 launched. Three models - Soul, Terra, Luna - gated to a partner list, at the government's request. OpenAI said publicly they don't want this to be permanent. They went along with it anyway.

What just appeared is a licensing regime for frontier AI. No public framework. No congressional approval. No articulated criteria. One person's discretion.

Both labs agreed to it, even if reluctantly.

The question this raises: if you're not on that approved list, what are you actually building on? And what happens when the gap between the gated tools and everything else keeps widening?

I don't have a clean answer. But "who controls access to the best AI" just became a real and open question - and most of us aren't on the list.
LI . NO. 02
76% of small businesses are using AI. 14% say they're actually seeing results.

That stat stopped me this morning.

The gap isn't the tool. The business owner who signed up for ChatGPT and the one getting real results are often running the exact same product.

The difference is how they came at it. One added a tool. The other rebuilt a workflow.

Adding a tool is easy. Five minutes. You sign up, poke around, it feels cool, and then you mostly go back to what you were already doing before.

Rebuilding a workflow is harder. It means getting specific: what's actually eating my time? Where does AI actually fit in that? Then doing the unsexy work of connecting it to how you actually operate.

The 62% stuck in the middle aren't bad at AI. They're just not asking the right question first.

What are you already doing for yourself that someone else could help you systematize?

Start there.
LI . NO. 03
Cursor just shipped a mobile app. You can now supervise AI coding agents from your phone.

That sentence is doing a lot of work. The word is "supervise."

Two years ago, building software meant sitting at a keyboard writing code. Now it means telling an agent what you want, watching it work, and catching it when it goes sideways.

The skill that matters most right now isn't writing code. It's knowing how to direct something that can write code.

Prompt clearly. Review the output well. Catch the mistakes early. That's the job.

And that's not just a developer thing. That's every knowledge worker over the next few years.

The person who can clearly articulate what they want, spot when the output is off, and give sharp feedback is going to outrun the person doing every step manually.

The job changed. Most job descriptions just haven't caught up yet.

What does "supervise" look like in your role?
